# Analysis: Power and people in enterprise architecture - Episode 68, Tetradian on Architectures

## Executive Summary
Power in enterprise architecture is defined as the ability to do work, which can be measured across forms like power-over, power-under, and dysfunctional competition, and mapped to dimensions of effectiveness such as efficient, reliable, and human-oriented. The speaker emphasizes indirect intervention tactics for power issues, especially avoiding direct confrontation in major conflicts, and highlights enterprise architecture as a politically challenging yet highly rewarding domain.[1][4][6]

## Key Takeaways
- Power is measurable on a scale (e.g., increasing or decreasing) and categorizable into forms like power-over, power-under, power-lost, incompetent, dysfunctional, and competition.[1][4]
- Map power dynamics to effectiveness dimensions: efficient, reliable, elegant, human-oriented, integrated, and on-purpose/appropriate, applicable to both technology systems and human relationships.[1][2]
- Systems enabling timely, trustworthy information enhance work ability, while unreliable ones destroy it, illustrating power's impact on productivity.[1]
- Use indirect tactics from resources like the book *Semper et Score* to address power problems without direct confrontation, which worsens major fights.[1]
- Enterprise architecture is inherently political and power-focused but offers the most rewarding work in technical and human senses.[1][3][6]

## Actionable Insights / Tutorials
- **Measure power**: Assess power forms (e.g., power-over where people prop up by putting others down) on a scale and track changes over time.[1][4]
- **Map to effectiveness**: Align power issues with dimensions like efficiency or reliability; for example, compare systems by whether they provide "all the information I need exactly when I need it" versus untrustworthy outputs.[1][2]
