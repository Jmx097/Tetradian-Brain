# Analysis: Enterprise architecture and coronavirus: Rules: Tetradian on Architectures

## Executive Summary
The video argues that rules during the coronavirus pandemic are fundamentally dictated by the virus's behavior, not human whim, emphasizing mutual responsibilities over self-interest to combat the "free-rider" problem. It draws parallels to enterprise architecture, where organizations must enforce reciprocal rules, roles, and responsibilities to maintain trust and functionality. Politicians and leaders who flout rules, like a British adviser, erode societal trust, risking widespread harm and mirroring organizational dysfunctions such as one-sided hierarchies.[5]

## Key Takeaways
- Rules in a virus-dominated environment are set by the virus itself, not politics or personal choice, underscoring the need for science-based adherence.[1][2][5]
- The "free-rider" problem—where individuals ignore rules expecting others to comply—destroys trust, especially when leaders like politicians break their own rules, leading to societal and organizational risks.[5]
- Enterprise architecture teaches that organizations are bounded by mutual rules, roles, and responsibilities; violations create dysfunctions like "in-superordination" (managers neglecting duties to staff).[5]
- Responsibilities are reciprocal, requiring interlocks and checks; one-sided rules foster abuse, theft, or violence in both society and organizations.[5]
- Wearing masks exemplifies "we-first" responsibility—protecting others, not just self—contrasting with "me-first" free-riders who undermine social contracts.[5]

## Actionable Insights / Tutorials
- Enforce **mutual responsibilities** in organizations by designing rules with interlocks, checks, and balances to prevent free-riding and ensure leaders model compliance.[5]
- Reframe contracts and terms as **trusts and commitments** rather than one-sided controls to build reciprocity and avoid dysfunctional hierarchies.[5]
- Address **in-superordination** by contextualizing insubordination within mutual duties—managers must understand and support staff to maintain organizational trust.[5]
- In crisis response (e.g., pandemics), prioritize science-derived rules and hold rule-makers accountable to preserve trust and prevent large-scale non-compliance.[5]
