# Analysis: Introduction to Five Elements and leadership - Tetradian on Tools For Change

## Executive Summary
The video applies the **Five Elements model** (Purpose, Scope, Plan, Action, Review) to leadership, expanding beyond traditional manager-leader distinctions to identify distinct leadership roles within each domain, between domains, and a central integrating role. It outlines **11 types of leadership** tied to an enterprise vision or story, emphasizing balanced guidance through change processes.[1][6]

## Key Takeaways
- **Five Elements domains** require specific leadership: Purpose (strategy/context), Scope (portfolio/program management/values), Plan (project management/planning), Action (real-time execution, e.g., foreman), Review (after-action assessment/lessons learned).[1][6]
- **Five transitional leaderships** bridge domains: Purpose-to-Scope (triggering action via values), Scope-to-Plan (defining policies/scope), Plan-to-Action (starting execution to avoid analysis paralysis), Action-to-Review (stopping and assessing), Review-to-Purpose (linking completions back to vision).[1]
- **Central leadership** holds the enterprise story/vision, balancing conflicts across domainsâ€”ideally the executive, but often needing another role.[1][2]
- Total of **11 distinct leadership types** (5 domain-specific + 5 transitional + 1 central), enabling situational leadership throughout projects and enterprise cycles.[1][2]
- Model draws from cyclic processes like Tuckman's stages, contrasting with linear management hierarchies.[1]

## Actionable Insights / Tutorials
- Map leadership needs to project phases: Start with **Purpose leadership** for vision/strategy; shift via **values** to Scope (portfolio management); use **project managers** for Plan creation; initiate **Action** with a "start event" to break planning loops; end with **after-action review** against success criteria; reconnect via completions to Purpose.[1][6]
- Assign roles explicitly: Use foremen or equivalents for Action's real-time immediacy; ensure Review leadership despite lacking distinct business roles; designate a central "story-holder" to resolve domain conflicts.[1]
- Apply recursively: Each domain team enacts its own Five Elements cycle; hold everything to an overarching **enterprise story**.[1]
